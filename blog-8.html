The Big Law Survival Guide: The 5 Most Common Mistakes Law Interns Make

Intern Blueprints • Pitfalls

Introduction: When you are on a short Vacation Scheme or internship, every single interaction and deliverable forms part of your evaluation. While everyone focuses on what to do to stand out, it is equally critical to know what to avoid.

Based on industry observations, here are the 5 most common pitfalls that law interns fall into—and exactly how you can dodge them to secure your Training Contract return offer. 👇

01 | Rushing the Final Delivery (Prioritizing Speed Over Quality) ⏱️

The Precision Valuation Trap

After staring at a complex document or a legal research memo for hours—or even days—the natural urge is to hit "Send" just to get it off your desk. This impatience is a major trap.

The Reality Check: In Big Law, seniors typically only pass work to interns when a deadline isn't immediate. They are evaluating your precision, not your speed.

The Strategy: In an internship, Quality >>> Speed. Force yourself to proofread your work multiple times. Read it backward if you have to. Ensure it is the absolute best representation of your capabilities before it reaches a senior's inbox.

02 | Suffering in Silence (Not Asking When Confused) 🤫

The Operational Cost of Guessing

Many senior associates are managing packed schedules and will expect you to be proactive about your learning. They won't hand-hold you through a task.

The Pitfall: Interns often guess when they are unsure, hoping the senior will just correct it later.

The Consequence: If you guess incorrectly and head down the wrong legal track, the senior has to rewrite the entire piece of work from scratch. They will instantly flag you as someone who adds to their workload rather than reducing it. If you have genuinely tried to find the answer and are still stuck, ask early.

03 | Asking Questions Without Doing the Homework First ❌

Don't Use Seniors as a Human Search Engine

While asking for clarification is encouraged, treating a senior associate like a human search engine is a critical mistake.

The Ultimate Taboo: Asking a question that could have been answered by a 30-second Google search is a massive red flag.

The Pro-Tip: Before approaching a senior, exhaust your resources. Check the firm's internal archives, databases, and document management systems for previous samples, templates, or precedents. When you do ask your question, frame it like this: "I am working on X. I have checked Y and Z to find a solution, but I hit a wall here. Could you guide on the next step?" Batch your questions together to respect their time.

04 | Saying "No" to Tasks Too Quickly (Poor Workload Management) 📥

The Strategic Advocacy Multiplier Advantage

While it is important to manage your workload to ensure high-quality output, turning down work from a senior you haven't collaborated with yet is an incredibly missed opportunity.

The Strategic Move: In a short internship, every new senior you work with represents an additional positive evaluation toward your return offer.

The Rule of Thumb: Unless your current supervisor has strictly forbidden you from taking on more work, try your best not to reject a new task. If pulling a bit of overtime can get it done to a high standard, accept the work. More exposure equals more advocates at the round-table review.

05 | Monopolizing Conversations During Social Events 🗣️

The Active Listening Ratio Blueprint

Some interns feel an intense pressure to perform during casual lunches, drinks, or networking events. They dominate the conversation, constantly pitching their past achievements or opinions.

The Pivot: Casual social events are designed for you to listen, learn about the firm's unedited culture, and build genuine, organic rapport.

The Golden Ratio: Let the seniors drive the conversation. Often, they have questions they want to ask you—such as your honest reflections on the internship or how you are finding the firm culture. Keep your speaking ratio at a modest balance, practice active listening, and show real curiosity about the people across the table.

Ending Note:

An internship isn't about being an absolute expert in the law on day one; it's about proving you are precise, teachable, reliable, and pleasant to work with at 2:00 AM. Avoid these five mistakes, and you are already ahead of half the competition.
