3 Things I Wish I Knew Before I Started My Big Law Training Contract

Workplace Boundaries • Longevity

Introduction: Securing a Training Contract (TC) is a massive achievement, and as law graduates, we are naturally wired to be hyper-responsible overachievers. But entering the relentless machine of Big Law requires a completely different survival strategy than surviving law school.

Reflecting on my own journey, here are 3 crucial things I desperately wish someone had told me before my first day on the job. 👇

01 | Protect Your Health Fiercely (You Are Not a Machine) 🩺

The Destructive Realities of Unchecked Deadlines

When a deal hits a major project milestone, it is incredibly easy to fall into the trap of believing that work matters more than anything else in your life.

The Breaking Point: I remember times when the sheer volume of urgent deadlines caused so much anxiety that I went 24 hours without eating a single thing. I felt so paralyzed by the clock that I didn't even dare to leave my desk for a bathroom break. At my lowest point, the stress was so overwhelming that I actively caught myself wishing I'd get into a minor car accident just to be hospitalized—so I would have a legitimate excuse not to go to the office.

The Reality Check: You are a human being, not a corporate machine. And frankly, even machines require scheduled downtime for maintenance. No matter how brutal your day is, you can always carve out 10 to 15 minutes to sit down and eat.

The Lesson: Never skip meals. Letting yourself get "hangry" severely degrades your emotional resilience, turning stress into pure anxiety and plunging you into a vicious cycle.

02 | Stop Trying to Be the "Nice Guy" (Learn the Art of Delegation) 🤝

Cracking the Workflow Management Equation

As a trainee, you will have the opportunity to lead interns and paralegals, as well as collaborate with legal secretaries and translation teams. This makes your TC a massive crash course in people management and workflow distribution.

The Trainee Trap: Early on, I frequently found myself working late into the night and handling tedious tasks myself simply because I felt bad about bothering the support staff or juniors so late.

The Harsh Lesson: I quickly realized that people don't actually know when you are secretly covering for them. They won't appreciate your silent sacrifice; instead, they will simply normalize it and expect it as a baseline going forward.

The Strategy: There is an established organizational theory that you should always delegate a task to the lowest-salaried person who is fully capable of executing it correctly. Every person in a law firm has a designated role. If your supervising partners empower you to manage a project, you must utilize the resources around you to maximize the overall efficiency of the team.

The Mindset Shift: Stop projecting your anxieties onto others. You do not need to feel immense guilt for sending an email late at night, nor do you need to overthink whether someone else is burning out. Everyone is a paid professional facing their own responsibilities and managing their own boundaries.

03 | Taking Annual Leave is a Fundamental Right, Not a Luxury ✈️

Flipping the Script on Leave Guilt

Many fresh graduates suffer from severe "leave guilt" or vacation anxiety. We trick ourselves into feeling deeply guilty for asking for time off, or we falsely assume that because we are new, we haven’t "earned" the right to rest yet.

Flip the Script: Annual Leave (AL) is not a favor granted by your firm; it is a core component of your compensation package, just like your medical insurance, MPF, or salary. It belongs to you.

The Logistical Reality: In a standard corporate job, you typically unlock your AL after passing your probation period. However, a Training Contract does not have a probation period. Generally, you can confidently request leave after your first three months on the job (though depending on your firm's specific culture, some teams are perfectly comfortable with you taking days off after your very first month).

Ending Note:

Your Training Contract is a marathon, and the habits you build during these two years will dictate your longevity as a qualified lawyer. Work hard, but never let the billable hours erase your humanity.
